1 Part 1: Defining the Research Problem

1.1 Research Questions and Hypotheses

Before any data analysis can begin, you must start with a clear research question. This is the foundation of your entire study.

Note

Simple analogy: Before you cook a meal, you decide what you want to make. The research question is your “meal”. You cannot collect data before you know what question you are answering.

1.1.1 What Makes a Good Research Question?

A good research question has three essential characteristics:

1. Simple

  • Contains one predictor variable and one outcome variable
  • More complex hypotheses with multiple predictors or outcomes should be broken into multiple simple hypotheses
  • Example: instead of studying all types of smoking (vape, cigarette, cigar, pipe), simplify to one variable — “smoking status” (smoker vs. non-smoker). This is called a combined predictor variable.

2. Specific

  • Leaves no ambiguity about the subjects or variables being studied
  • Clearly defines how statistical significance will be tested
  • Uses precise operational definitions that summarize:
    • The nature and source of the subjects
    • How variables will be measured
    • Whether variables are continuous or categorical

Professor’s note: “If you’re studying deep sea fisheries, you need to define what exactly is a deep sea fish — it might mean something different to different people.”

3. Stated in Advance

  • The hypothesis must be determined before examining the data
  • Prevents the temptation to test multiple hypotheses after seeing results
  • Minimizes the opportunity to “fish” for significant findings
Warning

Why this matters in practice: If your results are not significant, your supervisor might ask you to test something different. Being specific about your objectives before the study protects you from this. The Professor said: “Being clear about your objectives before you do a study is very important.”

1.2 Study Planning

1.2.1 Research Plan Components

Your research plan should document:

  1. Study Design: How will data be collected? (observational vs experimental)
  2. Target Population: Who or what will you study?
  3. Sample Size: How many subjects will you need?
  4. Predictor and Outcome Variables: What are you measuring and why?
Note

Observational vs Experimental data (asked in class):

  • Observational — you watch what happens without changing anything (e.g., observing what food people choose in a canteen)
  • Experimental — you manipulate something and measure the effect (e.g., giving one group a drug and another a placebo)

1.2.2 Conceptualizing the Target Population

Before collecting data:

  • Define the specific population you are interested in
  • Establish inclusion criteria: what characteristics define who belongs in your study?
  • Establish exclusion criteria: who should NOT be included?

Professor’s example: “All second-year students in MTU that are male, that smoke, and that have diabetes — that is your target population. You have to be as specific as possible.”

1.2.3 Sampling Strategy

Once you define your population, decide how to select subjects:

Probability Sampling (preferred for statistical inference)

  • Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected
  • Allows you to generalize findings to the larger population

Non-Probability Sampling

  • Subjects are selected based on convenience or specific criteria
  • Makes it harder to generalize results

1.3 Statistical Design Concepts

When designing your study, understand these key concepts:

Concept Definition
Response Variable The outcome you are measuring (what you want to explain)
Factor An independent variable in your study
Level The different values or categories of a factor
Treatment The specific condition applied to subjects
Replication Using multiple subjects per treatment group
Randomization Randomly assigning subjects to treatment groups
Blocking Grouping similar subjects to reduce variation
Factorial Design Studying multiple factors simultaneously
Main Effect The effect of one factor on the response
Interaction Effect How one factor’s effect depends on another factor

Professor’s example of interaction: “If you had gender and treatment as two factors, the main effects are gender and treatment individually. The interaction effect is: does gender change the effect of treatment?”

2 Part 2: Measuring Variables

2.1 Measurement Scales

Important⭐ EXAM TIP — Know the 4 measurement scales!

The Professor asked students about this in class. It appears in exercises and exams.

The type of measurement scale you use affects:

  • How precise your data is
  • What statistical tests you can perform
  • The statistical power of your analysis
  • Your ability to answer the research question

2.1.1 The 4 Types of Variable

DATA
├── Categorical (groups / labels)
│   ├── Nominal   → unordered groups     e.g. gender, blood type
│   └── Ordinal   → ordered groups       e.g. pain level: low / medium / high
└── Numerical (numbers)
    ├── Discrete  → counted values        e.g. number of children: 0, 1, 2 ...
    └── Continuous → measured values      e.g. height, weight, temperature
Variable type Best graph Best statistics Information content
Nominal Bar chart, frequency table Mode, proportions Low
Ordinal Bar chart Median, mode Low–Medium
Discrete Bar chart, table Counts, frequencies Medium
Continuous Histogram, boxplot, scatterplot Mean, standard deviation High

2.1.2 Why Continuous Variables Are Preferred

Professor’s exact words: “Continuous variables are preferred. They contain more information, which improves efficiency. The result is a study with more power and a smaller sample size.”

Key principle: Always collect data as continuous if possible, even if you might later categorize it. For example, collect exact age (continuous) rather than age groups (categorical) — you can always group later, but you cannot “un-group” data.

2.2 Measurement Quality: Precision and Accuracy

These two concepts are not the same and are equally important for valid research.

2.2.1 Precision

Definition: How consistently a measurement produces the same value when repeated.

  • Reflects reproducibility, reliability, and consistency
  • Affected by random error (chance variation)
  • Determines the statistical power of your study
  • Greater precision → greater power to detect effects at the same sample size

3 sources of random error (reduce precision):

  1. Observer variability — differences in how observers take measurements (skills, technique)
  2. Instrument variability — equipment affected by temperature, mechanical wear, calibration
  3. Subject variability — natural variation within subjects (e.g., heart rate changes throughout the day)

How to reduce random error: Repeat the experiment multiple times and average the results.

2.2.2 Accuracy

Definition: The degree to which a measurement actually represents what it is supposed to measure.

  • Reflects the validity of the measurement
  • Determined by systematic error (consistent bias in one direction)
  • Accuracy and precision are not necessarily linked

How to reduce systematic error: Calibrate instruments regularly; use controls.

2.2.3 The Relationship Between Precision and Accuracy

Think of throwing darts at a target:

Situation Meaning
🎯 All darts near centre, spread out Accurate but not precise
🎯 All darts clustered together, far from centre Precise but not accurate
🎯 All darts clustered at the centre Both — the ideal!
NotePractice Quiz (from class exercise)

Match each statement to either Precision or Accuracy:

  1. The degree to which a variable represents what it is supposed to represent → Accuracy
  2. The degree to which a variable has almost the same value when measured repeatedly → Precision
  3. Increases validity of conclusions → Accuracy
  4. Threatened by systematic error → Accuracy
  5. Threatened by random error → Precision
  6. Assessed using repeated measures → Precision

3 Part 3: Initial Data Analysis (EDA)

3.1 What is EDA?

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) is the critical first step after data collection. It includes:

  • Summarising and visualising data (graphs, tables)
  • Univariate profiling — examine each variable one at a time
  • Bivariate profiling — examine pairs of variables together
  • Missing data — are there gaps in the data?
  • Outliers — are there unusual values?
  • Check model assumptions — does the data meet model requirements?
  • Variable transformations — does the data need to be transformed?

3.2 The Anscombe’s Quartet Lesson

Important⭐ EXAM TIP — Classic exam topic. Know the lesson.

The Professor said: “You can see the differences [between datasets] only when you visualize them as a scatterplot.” Numbers alone can be deeply misleading.

Anscombe’s quartet is a famous example with 4 datasets that have nearly identical summary statistics:

Statistic All 4 datasets
Mean of X 9.00
Mean of Y 7.50
Standard deviation of X 3.16
Standard deviation of Y 1.94
Correlation coefficient (r) 0.82

You would think they are the same dataset. But they are not. When plotted as scatterplots:

  1. Dataset I → Normal linear relationship ✓
  2. Dataset II → Curved (non-linear) relationship
  3. Dataset III → Linear with one extreme outlier
  4. Dataset IV → Vertical cluster — one point drives the whole correlation
Warning

Critical lesson about the correlation coefficient:

The correlation coefficient only tells you two things:

  1. The direction of the relationship (positive or negative)
  2. The strength of the relationship (0 = none, 1 = perfect)

It cannot tell you if the relationship is linear. Only a graph can show that.

Professor’s exact words: “A lot of people just say ‘oh yeah, it’s linear’, but you can’t see if it’s linear because you can’t see it. You have to see it on a graph.”

3.3 Components of Initial Data Analysis

3.3.1 1. Summarising and Visualising Data

Numerical summaries:

  • Mean, median, standard deviation
  • Percentiles and quartiles
  • Min and max values

Visual methods:

  • Histograms (distribution shape)
  • Box plots (median, quartiles, outliers — “two for the price of one” said the Professor)
  • Scatter plots (relationships between variables)

3.3.2 2. Univariate Profiling

Examining each variable individually:

  • What is the distribution shape?
  • Are there outliers?
  • Is the variable skewed?
  • What is the range of values?

3.3.3 3. Bivariate Profiling

Examining relationships between pairs of variables:

  • Scatter plots for continuous variables
  • Cross-tabulations for categorical variables
  • Correlations between continuous variables

3.3.4 4. Data Quality Checks

  • Missing data extent and patterns
  • Outliers and unusual values
  • Data entry errors
  • Inconsistencies in the data

4 Part 4: Missing Data

Important⭐ EXAM TIP — This is a major topic. Exercise Sheet 1 is almost entirely about missing data.

Know the 4 steps and the 3 types (MCAR, MAR, MNAR) with examples. The Professor said he will add extra notes to Canvas.

4.1 Understanding Missing Data

Missing data occurs when values are not recorded for some variables in some observations. In R, missing values appear as NA.

4.1.1 Why Data Goes Missing

  • Experimental or procedural errors
  • Data entry mistakes (very common when going from paper to digital)
  • Survey respondents skipping questions
  • Equipment malfunction
  • Subject dropout in longitudinal studies

4.1.2 Why Missing Data Is a Problem

  1. Reduces sample size — fewer observations means less statistical power
  2. Can introduce bias — if missingness is related to the values themselves
  3. Complicates analysis — many statistical tests cannot handle missing values
  4. May require remediation before any analysis can proceed

Professor’s note: “Missing data is part of the data cleaning process. Before you even look at your means and graphs, you should look at missing data first.”

4.2 4 Steps to Address Missing Data

4.2.1 Step 1: Determine the TYPE of missing data

Type Description
Ignorable Expected — part of the study design. No action needed.
Not ignorable Caused by some known or unknown process. Action required.

Example of ignorable missing data: A questionnaire has a section only for parents. For households without children, this section will be blank — this is expected and ignorable.

4.2.2 Step 2: Determine the EXTENT of missing data

  • How much data is missing for each variable?
  • How many complete cases are there?
  • Is the amount small enough not to affect results?

Rule of thumb:

  • < 5% per variable → Simple deletion is acceptable
  • 5–30% per variable → Consider imputation
  • > 30% per variable → Consider removing the variable entirely
  • ≥ 50% per variable → Remove the variable from analysis

4.2.3 Step 3: Diagnose the RANDOMNESS of missing data

Important⭐ Know these 3 types with definitions AND examples

MCAR — Missing Completely At Random

  • The probability of missingness is the same for all observations
  • Causes of missing data are completely unrelated to the data
  • ✅ Easiest to handle: use complete cases or simple imputation

MAR — Missing At Random

  • Missingness depends on another observed variable, not the missing value itself
  • Example: Older respondents are more likely to skip the earnings question, but this depends only on age (which you observe)
  • ✅ Manageable: use imputation methods that borrow information from other variables

MNAR — Not Missing At Random (also called Informative Missing)

  • Missingness depends on the missing value itself
  • Example: People with very high income are less likely to report it
  • ❌ Hardest to handle: bias is very difficult to correct; must report it
Type What causes missingness? Detectable from data? Recommended approach
MCAR Pure chance Only by checking patterns Complete cases or impute
MAR Another observed variable Yes — compare groups Impute using other variables
MNAR The missing value itself No — needs external knowledge Report it; use domain knowledge

4.2.4 Step 4: Select the IMPUTATION METHOD

Imputation = estimating (filling in) a missing value using other information you have.

Method How it works Limitation
Mean imputation Replace with the variable’s average Can distort the distribution
Regression imputation Predict using a regression model May underestimate variability
Stochastic regression Regression + random noise Better than deterministic
k-Nearest Neighbours (kNN) Borrow value from similar observations More complex
LOCF (Last Observation Carried Forward) Use previous measurement (longitudinal data) Conservative — underestimates change
Multiple imputation Create multiple complete datasets Most sophisticated; accounts for uncertainty
Listwise deletion Remove all rows with any missing value Reduces sample size
Pairwise deletion Use all available data per calculation Can cause inconsistency

4.3 Practical Implementation in R

# Check for missing values
is.na(x)                         # TRUE if x is missing
sum(is.na(x))                    # Count missing values in x

# Remove / work with missing data
mean(x, na.rm = TRUE)            # Calculate mean, ignoring NAs
complete.cases(df)               # Identify rows with NO missing values
na.omit(df)                      # Remove rows with ANY missing values
df <- df[complete.cases(df), ]   # Keep only complete cases

# VIM package for visualisation and imputation
# library(VIM)
# histMiss(df)       # Histogram showing missing vs observed (red = missing)
# marginplot(df)     # Scatterplot with missing data patterns on margins
# irmi(df)           # Iterative robust model-based imputation

Scenario Classification Reason

5 Part 5: Outliers

5.1 What Are Outliers?

Definition: Outliers are observations with unusual combinations of characteristics that make them distinctly different from other observations. Typically, an observation with an unusually high or low value on a variable.

Warning

Do NOT automatically delete outliers! The Professor said: “Just don’t go ‘oh yeah, it’s an outlier, we’ll get rid of that’. You have to ask yourself, why is it there? Maybe it’s a valid outlier.”

5.2 Classifying Outliers

3 types of outliers:

Type What it means What to do
Procedural error A data entry mistake or equipment malfunction (e.g., blood pressure recorded as 2000 mmHg) Investigate and correct; delete if unfixable
Extraordinary event A real but exceptional circumstance (e.g., a Christmas sales spike) Keep it, but note it in your report
Extraordinary observation A valid but genuinely unusual data point — no clear explanation Investigate; may keep; requires domain expertise

5.3 Handling Outliers

Before taking any action:

  1. Verify the value — is it a real measurement or an error?
  2. Understand the cause — why is this observation unusual?
  3. Check implications — does removing it change your conclusions?
  4. Consider domain knowledge — is this observation valid in your field?

Options:

  • Keep — if it represents a real, valid observation
  • Investigate and correct — if it is a measurement or data entry error
  • Remove — only if it is a confirmed error with no way to verify
  • Analyse with and without — run both analyses and report the difference

6 Part 6: Testing Model Assumptions

6.1 Why Assumptions Matter

Before fitting a statistical model, the data must satisfy the model’s assumptions. Some models are less affected by violations — this is called robustness.

6.1.1 Key Assumptions for Linear Models

Important⭐ EXAM TIP — Know these 4 assumptions

1. Linearity

  • The relationship between predictor (X) and outcome (Y) is linear (a straight line)
  • Check with: scatter plot
  • Violation fix: transformation or non-linear models

2. Independence

  • Observations are independent of each other
  • Common violations: time series, repeated measures, paired data
  • Check with: study design review

3. Homoscedasticity (constant variance)

  • The variance of errors is constant across all levels of the predictor
  • Check with: scatter plot of residuals — spread should be uniform
  • Violation fix: transformation

4. Normality

  • The errors (residuals) follow a normal distribution
  • Check with: Q-Q plot (quantile-quantile plot) or histogram of residuals
  • Note: large sample sizes make this less critical (Central Limit Theorem)
  • Violation fix: transformation or non-parametric tests

Professor’s note: “Some models are less affected by violating certain assumptions — this is called robustness. For example, if equal variance is not met in a t-test, you can still do the t-test — it is just modified to account for unequal variance.”

6.2 Data Transformation

If your data violates assumptions, transformation can help by stabilising variance, making distributions more normal, or making relationships more linear.

6.2.1 Common Transformations

Transformation Use when R code
Standardise (Z-score) Normalising variables (x - mean(x)) / sd(x)
Square root Mild right skew sqrt(x)
Log Moderate–strong right skew, positive data log(x) or log10(x)
Square Non-linear (curved) relationship x^2
Reciprocal Severe right skew 1/x
Box-Cox Optimal transformation unknown MASS::boxcox()
Important

Back-transformation is essential. When you transform data, your results are in the transformed scale. You must convert them back to the original units for interpretation.

Example from class: Variance (σ²) is in squared units — hard to interpret. Taking the square root gives you standard deviation (σ), which is back in the original units. That is a back-transformation.

6.2.2 Important Considerations

  1. Interpretation changes — after transformation, you interpret results on the transformed scale
  2. Assumptions still apply — the transformation must result in assumptions being met
  3. Try trial and error — if one transformation does not work, try another
  4. Alternative approaches — non-parametric tests or robust methods may be better

7 Summary Points

Important⭐ Review these before the exam
  • ✅ Data analysis always follows 5 steps: Define → EDA → Fit → Interpret → Validate
  • ✅ A good hypothesis is simple, specific, and stated in advance
  • ✅ Continuous variables are always preferred — more information, more power, smaller sample needed
  • ✅ Precision = same value every time (affected by random error; increases power)
  • ✅ Accuracy = correct value (affected by systematic error; increases validity)
  • ✅ ALWAYS plot your data first — Anscombe’s Quartet proves that numbers alone can be deeply misleading
  • ✅ The correlation coefficient only tells you direction and strength — NOT whether the relationship is linear
  • ✅ Missing data has 3 types: MCAR, MAR, MNAR — each needs a different approach
  • ✅ Do not just delete missing data — understand why it is missing first
  • ✅ Do not just delete outliers — understand why they exist first
  • ✅ Check 4 assumptions for linear models: Linearity, Independence, Homoscedasticity, Normality
  • ✅ If assumptions fail → try a data transformation (log, square root, etc.) and back-transform results

**Observational vs Experimental data** (asked in class):

- **Observational** — you watch what happens without changing anything (e.g., observing what food people choose in a canteen)
- **Experimental** — you manipulate something and measure the effect (e.g., giving one group a drug and another a placebo)
:::

### Conceptualizing the Target Population

Before collecting data:

- Define the **specific population** you are interested in
- Establish **inclusion criteria**: what characteristics define who belongs in your study?
- Establish **exclusion criteria**: who should NOT be included?

> **Professor's example:** *"All second-year students in MTU that are male, that smoke, and that have diabetes — that is your target population. You have to be as specific as possible."*

### Sampling Strategy

Once you define your population, decide how to select subjects:

**Probability Sampling** (preferred for statistical inference)

- Every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected
- Allows you to generalize findings to the larger population

**Non-Probability Sampling**

- Subjects are selected based on convenience or specific criteria
- Makes it harder to generalize results

---

## Statistical Design Concepts

When designing your study, understand these key concepts:

| Concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Response Variable** | The outcome you are measuring (what you want to explain) |
| **Factor** | An independent variable in your study |
| **Level** | The different values or categories of a factor |
| **Treatment** | The specific condition applied to subjects |
| **Replication** | Using multiple subjects per treatment group |
| **Randomization** | Randomly assigning subjects to treatment groups |
| **Blocking** | Grouping similar subjects to reduce variation |
| **Factorial Design** | Studying multiple factors simultaneously |
| **Main Effect** | The effect of one factor on the response |
| **Interaction Effect** | How one factor's effect depends on another factor |

> **Professor's example of interaction:** *"If you had gender and treatment as two factors, the main effects are gender and treatment individually. The interaction effect is: does gender change the effect of treatment?"*

# Part 2: Measuring Variables

## Measurement Scales

::: {.callout-important}
## ⭐ EXAM TIP — Know the 4 measurement scales!
The Professor asked students about this in class. It appears in exercises and exams.
:::

The **type of measurement scale** you use affects:

- How precise your data is
- What statistical tests you can perform
- The statistical power of your analysis
- Your ability to answer the research question

### The 4 Types of Variable

```
DATA
├── Categorical (groups / labels)
│   ├── Nominal   → unordered groups     e.g. gender, blood type
│   └── Ordinal   → ordered groups       e.g. pain level: low / medium / high
└── Numerical (numbers)
    ├── Discrete  → counted values        e.g. number of children: 0, 1, 2 ...
    └── Continuous → measured values      e.g. height, weight, temperature
```

| Variable type | Best graph | Best statistics | Information content |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nominal | Bar chart, frequency table | Mode, proportions | Low |
| Ordinal | Bar chart | Median, mode | Low–Medium |
| Discrete | Bar chart, table | Counts, frequencies | Medium |
| **Continuous** | Histogram, boxplot, scatterplot | **Mean, standard deviation** | **High** |

### Why Continuous Variables Are Preferred

> **Professor's exact words:** *"Continuous variables are preferred. They contain more information, which improves efficiency. The result is a study with more power and a smaller sample size."*

**Key principle:** Always collect data as **continuous** if possible, even if you might later categorize it. For example, collect exact age (continuous) rather than age groups (categorical) — you can always group later, but you cannot "un-group" data.

## Measurement Quality: Precision and Accuracy

These two concepts are **not the same** and are equally important for valid research.

### Precision

**Definition:** How consistently a measurement produces the same value when repeated.

- Reflects **reproducibility, reliability, and consistency**
- Affected by **random error** (chance variation)
- Determines the **statistical power** of your study
- Greater precision → greater power to detect effects at the same sample size

**3 sources of random error (reduce precision):**

1. **Observer variability** — differences in how observers take measurements (skills, technique)
2. **Instrument variability** — equipment affected by temperature, mechanical wear, calibration
3. **Subject variability** — natural variation within subjects (e.g., heart rate changes throughout the day)

**How to reduce random error:** Repeat the experiment multiple times and average the results.

### Accuracy

**Definition:** The degree to which a measurement actually represents what it is **supposed to measure**.

- Reflects the **validity** of the measurement
- Determined by **systematic error** (consistent bias in one direction)
- Accuracy and precision are **not necessarily linked**

**How to reduce systematic error:** Calibrate instruments regularly; use controls.

### The Relationship Between Precision and Accuracy

Think of throwing darts at a target:

| Situation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 🎯 All darts near centre, spread out | Accurate but **not precise** |
| 🎯 All darts clustered together, far from centre | Precise but **not accurate** |
| 🎯 All darts clustered at the centre | **Both** — the ideal! |

::: {.callout-note}
## Practice Quiz (from class exercise)
Match each statement to either **Precision** or **Accuracy**:

1. The degree to which a variable represents what it is supposed to represent → ***Accuracy***
2. The degree to which a variable has almost the same value when measured repeatedly → ***Precision***
3. Increases validity of conclusions → ***Accuracy***
4. Threatened by systematic error → ***Accuracy***
5. Threatened by random error → ***Precision***
6. Assessed using repeated measures → ***Precision***
:::

# Part 3: Initial Data Analysis (EDA)

## What is EDA?

**Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)** is the critical first step after data collection. It includes:

- **Summarising and visualising** data (graphs, tables)
- **Univariate profiling** — examine each variable one at a time
- **Bivariate profiling** — examine pairs of variables together
- **Missing data** — are there gaps in the data?
- **Outliers** — are there unusual values?
- **Check model assumptions** — does the data meet model requirements?
- **Variable transformations** — does the data need to be transformed?

---

## The Anscombe's Quartet Lesson

::: {.callout-important}
## ⭐ EXAM TIP — Classic exam topic. Know the lesson.
The Professor said: *"You can see the differences [between datasets] only when you visualize them as a scatterplot."* Numbers alone can be deeply misleading.
:::

Anscombe's quartet is a famous example with **4 datasets** that have nearly **identical summary statistics**:

| Statistic | All 4 datasets |
|---|---|
| Mean of X | 9.00 |
| Mean of Y | 7.50 |
| Standard deviation of X | 3.16 |
| Standard deviation of Y | 1.94 |
| Correlation coefficient (r) | **0.82** |

You would think they are the same dataset. **But they are not.** When plotted as scatterplots:

1. **Dataset I** → Normal linear relationship ✓
2. **Dataset II** → Curved (non-linear) relationship
3. **Dataset III** → Linear with one extreme outlier
4. **Dataset IV** → Vertical cluster — one point drives the whole correlation

::: {.callout-warning}
**Critical lesson about the correlation coefficient:**

The correlation coefficient only tells you **two things**:

1. The **direction** of the relationship (positive or negative)
2. The **strength** of the relationship (0 = none, 1 = perfect)

It **cannot** tell you if the relationship is linear. Only a graph can show that.

> **Professor's exact words:** *"A lot of people just say 'oh yeah, it's linear', but you can't see if it's linear because you can't see it. You have to see it on a graph."*
:::

---

## Components of Initial Data Analysis

### 1. Summarising and Visualising Data

**Numerical summaries:**

- Mean, median, standard deviation
- Percentiles and quartiles
- Min and max values

**Visual methods:**

- Histograms (distribution shape)
- Box plots (median, quartiles, outliers — *"two for the price of one"* said the Professor)
- Scatter plots (relationships between variables)

### 2. Univariate Profiling

Examining each variable **individually**:

- What is the distribution shape?
- Are there outliers?
- Is the variable skewed?
- What is the range of values?

### 3. Bivariate Profiling

Examining relationships **between pairs** of variables:

- Scatter plots for continuous variables
- Cross-tabulations for categorical variables
- Correlations between continuous variables

### 4. Data Quality Checks

- **Missing data** extent and patterns
- **Outliers** and unusual values
- **Data entry errors**
- **Inconsistencies** in the data

# Part 4: Missing Data

::: {.callout-important}
## ⭐ EXAM TIP — This is a major topic. Exercise Sheet 1 is almost entirely about missing data.
Know the 4 steps and the 3 types (MCAR, MAR, MNAR) with examples. The Professor said he will add extra notes to Canvas.
:::

## Understanding Missing Data

**Missing data** occurs when values are not recorded for some variables in some observations. In R, missing values appear as `NA`.

### Why Data Goes Missing

- Experimental or procedural errors
- Data entry mistakes (very common when going from paper to digital)
- Survey respondents skipping questions
- Equipment malfunction
- Subject dropout in longitudinal studies

### Why Missing Data Is a Problem

1. **Reduces sample size** — fewer observations means less statistical power
2. **Can introduce bias** — if missingness is related to the values themselves
3. **Complicates analysis** — many statistical tests cannot handle missing values
4. **May require remediation** before any analysis can proceed

> **Professor's note:** *"Missing data is part of the data cleaning process. Before you even look at your means and graphs, you should look at missing data first."*

---

## 4 Steps to Address Missing Data

### Step 1: Determine the TYPE of missing data

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Ignorable** | Expected — part of the study design. No action needed. |
| **Not ignorable** | Caused by some known or unknown process. Action required. |

**Example of ignorable missing data:** A questionnaire has a section only for parents. For households without children, this section will be blank — this is *expected and ignorable*.

---

### Step 2: Determine the EXTENT of missing data

- How much data is missing for **each variable**?
- How many **complete cases** are there?
- Is the amount small enough not to affect results?

**Rule of thumb:**

- **< 5% per variable** → Simple deletion is acceptable
- **5–30% per variable** → Consider imputation
- **> 30% per variable** → Consider removing the variable entirely
- **≥ 50% per variable** → Remove the variable from analysis

---

### Step 3: Diagnose the RANDOMNESS of missing data

::: {.callout-important}
## ⭐ Know these 3 types with definitions AND examples
:::

**MCAR — Missing Completely At Random**

- The probability of missingness is the **same for all observations**
- Causes of missing data are **completely unrelated** to the data
- ✅ Easiest to handle: use complete cases or simple imputation

**MAR — Missing At Random**

- Missingness depends on **another observed variable**, not the missing value itself
- Example: Older respondents are more likely to skip the earnings question, but this depends only on *age* (which you observe)
- ✅ Manageable: use imputation methods that borrow information from other variables

**MNAR — Not Missing At Random** (also called Informative Missing)

- Missingness depends on the **missing value itself**
- Example: People with very high income are less likely to report it
- ❌ Hardest to handle: bias is very difficult to correct; must report it

| Type | What causes missingness? | Detectable from data? | Recommended approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| **MCAR** | Pure chance | Only by checking patterns | Complete cases or impute |
| **MAR** | Another observed variable | Yes — compare groups | Impute using other variables |
| **MNAR** | The missing value itself | No — needs external knowledge | Report it; use domain knowledge |

---

### Step 4: Select the IMPUTATION METHOD

**Imputation** = estimating (filling in) a missing value using other information you have.

| Method | How it works | Limitation |
|---|---|---|
| **Mean imputation** | Replace with the variable's average | Can distort the distribution |
| **Regression imputation** | Predict using a regression model | May underestimate variability |
| **Stochastic regression** | Regression + random noise | Better than deterministic |
| **k-Nearest Neighbours (kNN)** | Borrow value from similar observations | More complex |
| **LOCF** (Last Observation Carried Forward) | Use previous measurement (longitudinal data) | Conservative — underestimates change |
| **Multiple imputation** | Create multiple complete datasets | Most sophisticated; accounts for uncertainty |
| **Listwise deletion** | Remove all rows with any missing value | Reduces sample size |
| **Pairwise deletion** | Use all available data per calculation | Can cause inconsistency |

---

## Practical Implementation in R

```r
# Check for missing values
is.na(x)                         # TRUE if x is missing
sum(is.na(x))                    # Count missing values in x

# Remove / work with missing data
mean(x, na.rm = TRUE)            # Calculate mean, ignoring NAs
complete.cases(df)               # Identify rows with NO missing values
na.omit(df)                      # Remove rows with ANY missing values
df <- df[complete.cases(df), ]   # Keep only complete cases

# VIM package for visualisation and imputation
# library(VIM)
# histMiss(df)       # Histogram showing missing vs observed (red = missing)
# marginplot(df)     # Scatterplot with missing data patterns on margins
# irmi(df)           # Iterative robust model-based imputation
```

# Part 5: Outliers

## What Are Outliers?

**Definition:** Outliers are observations with **unusual combinations of characteristics** that make them distinctly different from other observations. Typically, an observation with an unusually high or low value on a variable.

::: {.callout-warning}
**Do NOT automatically delete outliers!**
The Professor said: *"Just don't go 'oh yeah, it's an outlier, we'll get rid of that'. You have to ask yourself, why is it there? Maybe it's a valid outlier."*
:::

---

## Classifying Outliers

**3 types of outliers:**

| Type | What it means | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| **Procedural error** | A data entry mistake or equipment malfunction (e.g., blood pressure recorded as 2000 mmHg) | Investigate and correct; delete if unfixable |
| **Extraordinary event** | A real but exceptional circumstance (e.g., a Christmas sales spike) | Keep it, but note it in your report |
| **Extraordinary observation** | A valid but genuinely unusual data point — no clear explanation | Investigate; may keep; requires domain expertise |

---

## Handling Outliers

Before taking any action:

1. **Verify the value** — is it a real measurement or an error?
2. **Understand the cause** — why is this observation unusual?
3. **Check implications** — does removing it change your conclusions?
4. **Consider domain knowledge** — is this observation valid in your field?

**Options:**

- **Keep** — if it represents a real, valid observation
- **Investigate and correct** — if it is a measurement or data entry error
- **Remove** — only if it is a confirmed error with no way to verify
- **Analyse with and without** — run both analyses and report the difference

# Part 6: Testing Model Assumptions

## Why Assumptions Matter

Before fitting a statistical model, the data must **satisfy the model's assumptions**. Some models are less affected by violations — this is called **robustness**.

### Key Assumptions for Linear Models

::: {.callout-important}
## ⭐ EXAM TIP — Know these 4 assumptions
:::

**1. Linearity**

- The relationship between predictor (X) and outcome (Y) is **linear** (a straight line)
- Check with: scatter plot
- Violation fix: transformation or non-linear models

**2. Independence**

- Observations are **independent** of each other
- Common violations: time series, repeated measures, paired data
- Check with: study design review

**3. Homoscedasticity** (constant variance)

- The **variance of errors is constant** across all levels of the predictor
- Check with: scatter plot of residuals — spread should be uniform
- Violation fix: transformation

**4. Normality**

- The **errors (residuals)** follow a normal distribution
- Check with: **Q-Q plot** (quantile-quantile plot) or histogram of residuals
- Note: large sample sizes make this less critical (Central Limit Theorem)
- Violation fix: transformation or non-parametric tests

> **Professor's note:** *"Some models are less affected by violating certain assumptions — this is called robustness. For example, if equal variance is not met in a t-test, you can still do the t-test — it is just modified to account for unequal variance."*

---

## Data Transformation

If your data **violates assumptions**, transformation can help by stabilising variance, making distributions more normal, or making relationships more linear.

### Common Transformations

| Transformation | Use when | R code |
|---|---|---|
| **Standardise** (Z-score) | Normalising variables | `(x - mean(x)) / sd(x)` |
| **Square root** | Mild right skew | `sqrt(x)` |
| **Log** | Moderate–strong right skew, positive data | `log(x)` or `log10(x)` |
| **Square** | Non-linear (curved) relationship | `x^2` |
| **Reciprocal** | Severe right skew | `1/x` |
| **Box-Cox** | Optimal transformation unknown | `MASS::boxcox()` |

::: {.callout-important}
**Back-transformation is essential.** When you transform data, your results are in the *transformed* scale. You must convert them back to the original units for interpretation.

**Example from class:** Variance (σ²) is in squared units — hard to interpret. Taking the square root gives you standard deviation (σ), which is back in the original units. *That is a back-transformation.*
:::

### Important Considerations

1. **Interpretation changes** — after transformation, you interpret results on the transformed scale
2. **Assumptions still apply** — the transformation must result in assumptions being met
3. **Try trial and error** — if one transformation does not work, try another
4. **Alternative approaches** — non-parametric tests or robust methods may be better
