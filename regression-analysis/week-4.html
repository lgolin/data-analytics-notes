Week 4

Experimental Design, Effect Size, Sample Size & Power

Note📝 Personal Annotations

Add your own notes here as you review this material.

1 Key Topics

This class covered two connected areas:

  • Fundamentals of Experimental Design — how to plan a well-structured study
  • Effect Size, Sample Size & Statistical Power — how these three quantities interact
  • Association vs. Causation — a critical concept in data analysis
  • Confounding Variables & Extraneous Variables — sources of bias in studies
  • ANOVA (preview) — extension of t-tests to multiple group means
  • Power Analysis in R — using the pwr package to calculate power and required sample size

2 Core Explanations

2.1 What is Experimental Design?

Experimental design is the process of planning a study so that you collect the right data, in the right amount, using the right methods. The goal is to produce valid and objective conclusions.

Before you run a single test, you need:

  1. A clear research question that can be converted into testable hypotheses (H₀ and Hₐ).
  2. An analysis plan — which variables will you measure? Are they categorical or numeric? What statistical test is appropriate?
  3. A predetermined significance level (usually α = 0.05). Never change this after looking at the data.
  4. Awareness of p-hacking — do not hunt through variables or analyses until you find a significant result. A null result is still a valid and publishable result.

2.1.1 The Three Principles of Efficient Experimental Design

Principle What it means
Randomisation Every experimental unit has an equal chance of being assigned to any treatment group. This eliminates selection bias.
Blocking Group similar experimental units together before randomising. This reduces the effect of known sources of variability (similar to stratified random sampling).
Replication Repeat each experimental condition independently so that variability can be estimated. Large samples reduce random variation and let us detect small differences.

2.2 Experimental Studies vs. Observational Studies

Feature Experimental Study Observational Study
Researcher role Intervenes / manipulates conditions Only observes existing conditions
Randomisation Possible Not possible
Confounding Can be controlled via design Must be controlled statistically
Example Clinical trial (drug vs placebo) Survey on coffee and heart disease
TipQuick Rule

If you are unsure whether a study is experimental or observational, ask: Did the researcher set the conditions under which the data were collected? If yes → experimental. If no → observational.

2.2.1 Two Sources of Problems in Observational Studies

  1. Non-random selection — If participants are not selected randomly, the sample may not represent the population.
  2. Uncontrolled extraneous variables — Variables you did not account for that affect the outcome.

2.3 Association vs. Causation

“Association is not causation.”

Two variables are associated (or correlated) when they move together in a systematic way — as X increases, Y tends to increase (or decrease). However, this does not mean X causes Y.

2.3.1 Types of Association

Type Description Example
Statistical association Variables are correlated; no causation implied X and Y are correlated
Causal association One variable directly causes a change in the other Sugar consumption → tooth decay
Non-causal association A third variable explains the relationship between X and Y Confounding (see below)
Chance association (spurious) The relationship is a coincidence Ice cream sales & drownings — both rise in summer due to weather

2.3.2 Common Measures of Association

  • Pearson’s correlation coefficient (r) — parametric, measures linear association
  • Spearman’s rank correlation — non-parametric, based on ranked data (uses median)
  • Chi-square test — for categorical variables
  • Odds ratio / Relative risk — used in logistic regression

2.4 Extraneous and Confounding Variables

2.4.1 Extraneous Variables

Variables other than the independent variable that may affect the result. They can be:

  • Controlled — accounted for in the study design
  • Uncontrolled — not controlled, and potentially problematic

2.4.2 Confounding Variables

An uncontrolled extraneous variable that:

  • Influences both the independent and the dependent variable
  • Makes it impossible to determine which variable is truly causing the observed effect

Key rule: All confounding variables are extraneous variables, but not all extraneous variables are confounding.

NoteExample: Alcohol, Age, and HDL Cholesterol

A study examines the effect of alcohol consumption on HDL (good) cholesterol in males. The observed association could be confounded by age — because age independently affects cholesterol levels. We cannot separate the effect of alcohol from the effect of age.

In a linear model, age would be included and held constant to control for this confounding effect:

\[\text{HDL} = \alpha + \beta_1 \cdot \text{Alcohol} + \beta_2 \cdot \text{Age} + \varepsilon\]

2.4.3 Spurious Correlation

A spurious correlation is when two variables appear associated but there is no logical or causal link — the association is a coincidence, often caused by a hidden third variable.

Classic example: Ice cream sales and drowning rates are both correlated — but both are driven by hot weather, not by each other.

2.5 Key Terms in Experimental Design

Term Definition
Experimental unit The subject/item on which an experiment is performed (e.g., patients, animals, lab samples)
Factor A controlled explanatory variable whose levels are set by the experimenter
Level Each distinct setting of a factor (e.g., “Drug A”, “Drug B”, “Drug C”)
Treatment A specific combination of factor levels applied to an experimental unit
Response variable The outcome being measured (dependent variable / Y)
Explanatory variable The variable(s) used to explain the outcome (independent variable / X)
NoteExample: Drug + Vitamin C Study

“Six groups of patients are each treated with one of 3 drugs, either combined with Vitamin C or alone.”

  • Experimental units: Patients
  • Factors: Type of drug (3 levels) and Vitamin C (2 levels: yes/no)
  • Treatments: 6 (= 2 × 3 combinations)
  • Design: Factorial design → analysed using two-way ANOVA

2.6 Randomised Designs and Blind Studies

2.6.1 Completely Randomised Design (CRD)

All experimental units are randomly assigned to treatment groups. Every unit has an equal chance of receiving any treatment. This eliminates selection bias.

2.6.2 Randomised Block Design (RBD)

When known differences between units exist (e.g., gender), units are first divided into homogeneous blocks, then randomly assigned to treatment groups within each block. This reduces noise from that known variable.

  • Complete block: Every block contains every treatment.
  • Incomplete block: Not all treatments appear in every block (adjusted statistically).

2.6.3 Blind Studies (Reducing Bias Further)

Design Who doesn’t know the treatment assignment
Single blind Participants only
Double blind Participants and researchers administering the treatment
Triple blind Participants, researchers, and the data analysts

2.7 ANOVA — Preview

ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) extends the two-sample t-test to compare two or more means simultaneously.

Type Independent Variables Dependent Variable
One-way ANOVA 1 1
Two-way ANOVA 2 1

2.7.1 Hypotheses (e.g., one-way ANOVA with 3 groups)

\[H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2 = \mu_3\] \[H_A: \text{at least one mean is different}\]

Warning

ANOVA tells you if a difference exists between groups — not where the difference lies. A post-hoc analysis (e.g., Tukey’s test) is needed to identify which specific groups differ.

ANOVA partitions total variation into:

  • Explained variation — variation due to the treatment/design
  • Unexplained variation (error) — inherent variation within groups

2.8 Effect Size, Sample Size, and Statistical Power

These three quantities are deeply interconnected. Understanding their relationship is essential for designing studies and interpreting results.

2.8.1 Effect Size (Cohen’s d)

Effect size measures the practical significance of a difference — how large the difference between two means is relative to the variability in the data.

\[d = \frac{\mu_A - \mu_0}{\sigma}\]

Where:

  • \(\mu_A\) = mean under the alternative hypothesis
  • \(\mu_0\) = mean under the null hypothesis
  • \(\sigma\) = population standard deviation

A larger effect size = easier to detect a real difference.

2.8.2 Statistical Power (1 − β)

Power is the probability of correctly rejecting the null hypothesis when it is actually false.

\[\text{Power} = 1 - \beta = P(\text{Reject } H_0 \mid H_0 \text{ is false})\]

  • Low power → high risk of a Type II error (missing a real effect)
  • Target power is typically 80% or 90% in practice

2.8.3 The Relationship Between Effect Size, Sample Size, and Power

If you… Then power…
Increase sample size Increases
Increase effect size Increases
Decrease variability (σ) Increases
Use a one-tailed instead of two-tailed test Increases
Decrease α (e.g., from 0.05 to 0.01) Decreases
TipThe key insight from the simulation exercise

As sample size increases, sample means converge to the true population means, and p-values generally decrease — making it easier to detect a true difference. However, with a very small effect size, you may need an enormous sample to achieve significance.

3 Key Equations / Formulas

3.0.1 Cohen’s d (Effect Size for t-tests)

\[d = \frac{\mu_A - \mu_0}{\sigma}\]

3.0.2 Statistical Power

\[\text{Power} = 1 - \beta\]

3.0.3 Standard Error of the Mean

\[SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\]

3.0.4 General Linear Model (Multiple Regression / Observational)

\[Y = \alpha + \beta_1 X_1 + \beta_2 X_2 + \varepsilon\]

Where \(\varepsilon\) is the random error term.

3.0.5 ANOVA Decomposition

\[SS_{\text{Total}} = SS_{\text{Between}} + SS_{\text{Within}}\]

4 Practical Examples

4.1 Simulation Exercise (Worksheet 2)

The worksheet uses simulated data to explore how sample size affects p-values and hypothesis test conclusions.

4.1.1 Setup in R

set.seed(1)   # ensures reproducibility of "random" numbers

The rnorm(n, μ, σ) function generates n pseudo-random numbers from a Normal distribution with mean μ and standard deviation σ.

4.1.2 Four Cases Compared

Case Sample A Sample B Effect (difference of means) SD
1 μ=0, σ=4 μ=1, σ=4 Large (1.0) 4
2 μ=0, σ=4 μ=0.1, σ=4 Small (0.1) 4
3 μ=0, σ=1 μ=0.1, σ=1 Small (0.1) 1 (less variability)
4 μ=0, σ=10 μ=0.2, σ=10 Very small (0.2) 10 (more variability)

Key observation: For a given effect size, you need more observations when variability is higher. For a very small effect size, even thousands of observations may be needed.

# Example: Case 1 — testing two samples of size 50
A_50 <- rnorm(50, 0, 4)
B_50 <- rnorm(50, 1, 4)
t.test(A_50, B_50)

4.2 Power Analysis in R — pwr Package

install.packages("pwr")
library(pwr)

4.2.1 Key pwr Functions

Function Use case
pwr.t.test One sample, two sample, or paired t-test
pwr.anova.test Balanced one-way ANOVA
pwr.chisq.test Chi-square test
pwr.2p.test Two proportions (equal n)
pwr.r.test Correlation
pwr.f2.test General linear model

For each function you provide three of: sample size, effect size, significance level, power — and the fourth is calculated.

4.2.2 Worked Example: Chemical Company

A government inspector tests whether a chemical product contains more than 4.0 ml of caustic material (the safe limit). She takes n = 30 samples; σ = 1.3; the true mean may be as high as 4.35.

Effect size:

\[d = \frac{4.35 - 4.0}{1.3} = 0.2692\]

Power calculation in R:

pwr.t.test(n = 30, d = 0.2692, sig.level = 0.05,
           type = "one.sample", alternative = "greater")

Result: Power = 0.419 → only a 41.9% chance of detecting the effect with this sample size. The sample is underpowered.

4.3 Exercise 1: Height Study

Scenario: Testing whether the mean height of students at CIT exceeds 160 cm.

\[H_0: \mu = 160 \text{ cm}, \quad H_A: \mu > 160 \text{ cm}\]

Sample: n = 25, \(\bar{x}\) = 173.4 cm, s = 9

Type I Error: Reject H₀ and conclude that mean height > 160 cm when it is not.

Type II Error: Fail to reject H₀ and conclude mean height ≤ 160 cm when it actually is greater.

Power (if true μ = 165):

pwr.t.test(n = 25, d = 5/9, sig.level = 0.05,
           type = "one.sample", alternative = "greater")
# Power ≈ 85%

Two-tailed version (same μ = 165):

pwr.t.test(n = 25, d = 5/9, sig.level = 0.05,
           type = "one.sample")
# Power ≈ 76%

Conclusion: A one-tailed test is more powerful than a two-tailed test for a given effect size, sample size, and α — because all of the rejection region is on one side.

4.4 Exercise 2: Light Bulb Lifespan

Scenario: A manufacturer claims bulbs last 850 hours on average (σ = 50). A consumer group believes the true mean is only 810 hours.

\[H_0: \mu = 850, \quad H_A: \mu < 850\]

Required sample size for 90% power:

pwr.t.test(d = (810 - 850) / 50, power = 0.9,
           sig.level = 0.05, type = "one.sample",
           alternative = "less")
# n ≈ 15 (round up)

Simulation to verify (10,000 tests):

pvalues <- rep(0, 10000)
for (i in 1:10000) {
  sA <- rnorm(15, 810, 50)
  pvalues[i] <- t.test(sA, y = NULL, "less", 850)$p.value
}
sum(pvalues < 0.05)   # should be approximately 9,000 (90%)

5 Summary Points

  • Experimental design ensures that data is collected efficiently and that conclusions are valid. The three core principles are randomisation, blocking, and replication.

  • The key distinction between experimental and observational studies is whether the researcher manipulates conditions. Only experimental studies allow strong causal conclusions.

  • Association ≠ causation. Correlation measures how two variables move together; it does not prove that one causes the other.

  • Extraneous variables are any variables not of direct interest that may affect the outcome. When uncontrolled, they can become confounding variables — making it impossible to isolate the true cause of an effect.

  • Spurious correlations appear real but are coincidental, often driven by an unobserved third variable (e.g., ice cream sales and drowning rates both driven by summer weather).

  • ANOVA compares two or more means and partitions variation into between-group (explained) and within-group (unexplained) components. A post-hoc test is needed to identify which specific pairs of means differ.

  • Effect size (d) = the standardised difference between means. Larger effect → easier to detect with fewer observations.

  • Power = probability of correctly detecting a real effect. It increases with larger n, larger effect size, lower variability, and one-tailed tests.

  • You need three of four quantities (n, d, α, power) to calculate the fourth using pwr.t.test() in R.

  • “P is low, null must go” — reject H₀ when p-value < α (0.05). Otherwise, fail to reject H₀.

The rnorm(n, μ, σ) function generates n pseudo-random numbers from a Normal distribution with mean μ and standard deviation σ.

9.1.2 Four Cases Compared

Case Sample A Sample B Effect (difference of means) SD
1 μ=0, σ=4 μ=1, σ=4 Large (1.0) 4
2 μ=0, σ=4 μ=0.1, σ=4 Small (0.1) 4
3 μ=0, σ=1 μ=0.1, σ=1 Small (0.1) 1 (less variability)
4 μ=0, σ=10 μ=0.2, σ=10 Very small (0.2) 10 (more variability)

Key observation: For a given effect size, you need more observations when variability is higher. For a very small effect size, even thousands of observations may be needed.

Function Use case

For each function you provide three of: sample size, effect size, significance level, power — and the fourth is calculated.

9.2.2 Worked Example: Chemical Company

A government inspector tests whether a chemical product contains more than 4.0 ml of caustic material (the safe limit). She takes n = 30 samples; σ = 1.3; the true mean may be as high as 4.35.

Effect size:

\[d = \frac{4.35 - 4.0}{1.3} = 0.2692\]

Power calculation in R:

pwr.t.test(n = 30, d = 0.2692, sig.level = 0.05,
           type = "one.sample", alternative = "greater")

Result: Power = 0.419 → only a 41.9% chance of detecting the effect with this sample size. The sample is underpowered.

9.3 Exercise 1: Height Study

Scenario: Testing whether the mean height of students at CIT exceeds 160 cm.

\[H_0: \mu = 160 \text{ cm}, \quad H_A: \mu > 160 \text{ cm}\]

Sample: n = 25, \(\bar{x}\) = 173.4 cm, s = 9

Type I Error: Reject H₀ and conclude that mean height > 160 cm when it is not.

Type II Error: Fail to reject H₀ and conclude mean height ≤ 160 cm when it actually is greater.

Power (if true μ = 165):

pwr.t.test(n = 25, d = 5/9, sig.level = 0.05,
           type = "one.sample", alternative = "greater")
# Power ≈ 85%

Two-tailed version (same μ = 165):

pwr.t.test(n = 25, d = 5/9, sig.level = 0.05,
           type = "one.sample")
# Power ≈ 76%

Conclusion: A one-tailed test is more powerful than a two-tailed test for a given effect size, sample size, and α — because all of the rejection region is on one side.

9.4 Exercise 2: Light Bulb Lifespan

Scenario: A manufacturer claims bulbs last 850 hours on average (σ = 50). A consumer group believes the true mean is only 810 hours.

\[H_0: \mu = 850, \quad H_A: \mu < 850\]

Required sample size for 90% power:

pwr.t.test(d = (810 - 850) / 50, power = 0.9,
           sig.level = 0.05, type = "one.sample",
           alternative = "less")
# n ≈ 15 (round up)

Simulation to verify (10,000 tests):

pvalues <- rep(0, 10000)
for (i in 1:10000) {
  sA <- rnorm(15, 810, 50)
  pvalues[i] <- t.test(sA, y = NULL, "less", 850)$p.value
}
sum(pvalues < 0.05)   # should be approximately 9,000 (90%)

10 Summary Points

  • Experimental design ensures that data is collected efficiently and that conclusions are valid. The three core principles are randomisation, blocking, and replication.

  • The key distinction between experimental and observational studies is whether the researcher manipulates conditions. Only experimental studies allow strong causal conclusions.

  • Association ≠ causation. Correlation measures how two variables move together; it does not prove that one causes the other.

  • Extraneous variables are any variables not of direct interest that may affect the outcome. When uncontrolled, they can become confounding variables — making it impossible to isolate the true cause of an effect.

  • Spurious correlations appear real but are coincidental, often driven by an unobserved third variable (e.g., ice cream sales and drowning rates both driven by summer weather).

  • ANOVA compares two or more means and partitions variation into between-group (explained) and within-group (unexplained) components. A post-hoc test is needed to identify which specific pairs of means differ.

  • Effect size (d) = the standardised difference between means. Larger effect → easier to detect with fewer observations.

  • Power = probability of correctly detecting a real effect. It increases with larger n, larger effect size, lower variability, and one-tailed tests.

  • You need three of four quantities (n, d, α, power) to calculate the fourth using pwr.t.test() in R.

  • “P is low, null must go” — reject H₀ when p-value < α (0.05). Otherwise, fail to reject H₀.
